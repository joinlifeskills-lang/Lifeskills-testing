There is a phrase that has quietly transformed how we understand psychological suffering: *the body keeps the score*. Made famous by psychiatrist Bessel van der Kolk's landmark research, it describes something that many people intuitively sense but rarely have language for — that unprocessed stress, grief, and trauma don't simply vanish with time. They settle into the body. They live in the tightness of a chest that never fully releases, in the jaw that aches from years of clenching, in the shoulders that creep toward the ears the moment life gets hard.

But here is the part that doesn't always make it into the conversation: the body also holds the capacity to heal itself. And that process, while it can be guided and supported, ultimately requires you to show up for it.

## What Somatic Work Actually Is

"Somatic" comes from the Greek *soma*, meaning body. Somatic therapy is an umbrella term for approaches that treat psychological distress through the body rather than — or in addition to — the mind.

Where traditional talk therapy focuses on insight and understanding, somatic work focuses on sensation. On noticing. On the felt experience of being alive in your body right now.

Approaches within this field include:

- **Somatic Experiencing (SE)** — developed by Dr. Peter Levine, based on the observation that animals in the wild discharge stress through trembling and shaking, while humans tend to suppress these impulses, causing stress to remain locked in the nervous system
- **Sensorimotor Psychotherapy** — integrates mindfulness, body awareness, and cognitive approaches to process trauma stored below the level of conscious thought
- **Trauma-Sensitive Yoga** — uses physical postures not for fitness, but as a vehicle for safely re-inhabiting parts of the body that have been numbed or dissociated
- **Breathwork with somatic awareness** — uses conscious breathing to surface and process physiological stress responses that talking alone cannot reach

What unites all of these is a simple premise: **the path to healing runs through the body, not around it**.

## The Neuroscience Behind It

When a threatening event occurs — whether a single acute trauma or years of chronic stress — the brain's alarm system (the amygdala) encodes the experience at a physiological level before the rational mind has a chance to process it. This is why trauma responses so often feel irrational: *I know I'm safe, but my body doesn't believe it.*

The nervous system can become stuck in states of hyper-arousal (anxiety, hypervigilance, reactivity) or hypo-arousal (numbness, disconnection, fatigue). These are not character flaws. They are adaptive responses that have outlived their usefulness.

Research by Dr. Stephen Porges — whose Polyvagal Theory has reshaped trauma treatment — shows that the vagus nerve plays a central role in regulating these states. Crucially, somatic interventions that work with breath, movement, and interoception (awareness of internal bodily sensation) directly influence vagal tone. This is why a body-based practice can shift you out of a stress response faster than analysis alone.

A 2017 meta-analysis published in *Psychological Trauma* found that body-oriented therapies produced significant reductions in PTSD symptoms, with effects comparable to established cognitive approaches — and in some populations, stronger results in retention and completion rates.

The body is not a metaphor. It is a mechanism. And it responds to being worked with directly.

## The Part No One Wants to Hear: Responsibility

Here is where the conversation gets harder — and more important.

Somatic healing is not something that is done *to* you. A practitioner can create conditions for healing. They can hold space, offer tools, track your nervous system, and guide you back toward safety and sensation. But they cannot do the healing on your behalf.

This matters because one of the most seductive narratives in the wellness world is that healing is something you receive. A treatment. A session. A retreat. But while these things can catalyse change, the change itself requires your active participation. It requires you to choose — again and again — to turn toward your experience rather than away from it.

Viktor Frankl, writing from his experience in Nazi concentration camps, articulated this with devastating clarity: between stimulus and response, there is a space. And in that space lies our freedom and our responsibility. We do not choose what happens to us. We do choose how we respond.

In somatic terms, this means:

**You are responsible for noticing.** Not analysing, not fixing — just noticing. What does your body feel like right now? Where is there tension? Where is there ease? This is a practice that begins with 30 seconds of honest attention.

**You are responsible for showing up.** The benefits of somatic work are cumulative. One session can open a door. Consistent practice walks you through it. This requires a commitment to returning, even when — especially when — the work feels uncomfortable.

**You are responsible for your own window of tolerance.** This is the term practitioners use for the zone in which you are activated enough to do meaningful work, but not so overwhelmed that you shut down. Knowing your limits, communicating them, and gradually expanding them over time is part of what it means to take ownership of your healing.

**You are responsible for integration.** What happens between sessions matters as much as what happens during them. Journalling, rest, gentle movement, breathing practices, and honest reflection are how insights become embodied rather than simply understood.

## Self-Responsibility Is Not Self-Blame

This is a crucial distinction. Taking responsibility for your healing does not mean you are to blame for what happened to you. It does not mean you should be further along by now, or that seeking support is weakness, or that struggle is a moral failing.

It means recognising that you are the only one who lives inside your body. That no one else can breathe for you, move for you, or feel for you. That while circumstances may have shaped your nervous system, you are not permanently at their mercy.

There is a particular kind of freedom that comes from this recognition. When you stop waiting to be healed and start participating in your own recovery, something shifts. Agency returns. Not all at once, and not without support — but it returns.

## What a Somatic Practice Looks Like in Daily Life

You do not need to be in formal therapy to begin working somatically. Here are practices grounded in research that you can start today:

**Body check-ins (2 minutes, 3x daily)**
Pause. Close your eyes or soften your gaze. Scan slowly from head to feet. Notice — without judgement — where you feel sensation, tension, warmth, numbness, or ease. Name what you find. This is interoception training, and it builds over time.

**Shake and discharge**
Stand and allow your body to shake gently — legs, arms, torso. This mimics the natural discharge mechanism that other mammals use after threat. It feels strange at first. Do it anyway. Even 60 seconds can meaningfully reduce cortisol.

**Orienting**
When you feel activated or anxious, slowly look around the room. Let your eyes rest on objects. Notice colours, shapes, textures. This activates the ventral vagal pathway — the branch of the nervous system associated with safety and social connection — and interrupts the stress response.

**Grounding through pressure**
Press your feet firmly into the floor. Press your back into your chair. Feel the physical support of the surface beneath you. This simple act of noticing containment can interrupt a spiral of anxious thought by anchoring you in the present moment of your body.

**Extended exhale breathing**
Inhale for 4 counts. Exhale for 8. Repeat for 5 minutes. The extended exhale stimulates the vagus nerve and shifts your nervous system from sympathetic to parasympathetic dominance. This is not relaxation advice — it is applied physiology.

## You Are Worth the Effort

Perhaps the deepest dimension of somatic self-responsibility is this: it requires you to believe that your wellbeing matters enough to tend to it. For many people, this is not a given. Years of overperformance, self-neglect, trauma, or simply the relentless demands of modern life can erode that belief quietly and thoroughly.

Taking responsibility for your healing is, at its most fundamental level, an act of self-respect. A declaration that you are worth paying attention to. That the sensations in your body deserve acknowledgement. That you — exactly as you are, exactly where you are — are worth showing up for.

The body has been keeping score all along. It is time to pick up the pen.
